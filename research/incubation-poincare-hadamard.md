## 1. Poincaré's Fuchsian-functions narrative — the four documented episodes

The whole point, Poincaré tells his audience, is not the theorems but **"the
circumstances"** (VERBATIM, Halsted). He gives one worked example, his first memoir on
Fuchsian functions, in four beats. All four are the raw data the rest of the theory is
induced from — so they are receipts, not anecdote.

**(a) Fifteen days of conscious failure.** PARAPHRASE (Halsted, *Monist* p. 326): for
fifteen days he tried to prove no such functions could exist; every day he sat an hour or
two at his work table, tried "a great number of combinations," and reached no result.

**(b) The black-coffee night.** One evening, against his custom, he drank black coffee
and could not sleep — VERBATIM (Halsted): **"Ideas rose in crowds; I felt them collide
until pairs interlocked."** By morning he had established a class of Fuchsian functions
and "had only to write out the results." This is his one direct look at the unconscious
combinatory process made partly conscious.

**(c) The omnibus step at Coutances.** On a geologic excursion, travel having made him
forget the mathematics — VERBATIM (Halsted): **"At the moment when I put my foot on the
step the idea came to me,"** that his transformations were identical with those of
non-Euclidean geometry. Critically for the plugin, VERBATIM: **"I did not verify the
idea… but I felt a perfect certainty."** And then, VERBATIM: **"for conscience' sake I
verified the result at my leisure."** Illumination and mandatory later verification, in
one paragraph.

**(d) The seaside walk.** Disgusted with failure on some arithmetic questions, he went to
the seaside and thought of something else; one morning, walking on the bluff, an idea came
"with just the same characteristics of brevity, suddenness, and immediate certainty"
(VERBATIM, Halsted) — that the arithmetic transformations of indeterminate ternary
quadratic forms were identical with those of non-Euclidean geometry.

**(e) The Mont-Valérien moment.** Having gone to do military service and so "very
differently occupied," one day going along the street the solution to the one obstacle
that had held out "suddenly appeared to me" (VERBATIM, Halsted). He wrote the final memoir
"at a single stroke and without difficulty."

## 2. Poincaré's explicit rules (his own, stated as conclusions)

**Rule 1 — illumination signals prior unconscious work.** VERBATIM (Halsted, p. 328):
**"Most striking at first is this appearance of sudden illumination, a manifest sign of
long, unconscious prior work."**

**Rule 2 — the unconscious must be bracketed by conscious work.** This is the load-bearing
one. VERBATIM (Halsted, p. 328): unconscious work **"is possible, and of a certainty it is
only fruitful, if it is on the one hand preceded and on the other hand followed by a period
of conscious work."** PARAPHRASE of the surrounding argument: the sudden inspirations never
come except after days of voluntary effort that "appeared absolutely fruitless"; those
efforts "set agoing the unconscious machine," and the second conscious period is needed to
put the result in shape, deduce consequences, and — "above all" — verify.

**Rule 3 — verification is mandatory because certainty can deceive.** PARAPHRASE (Halsted,
pp. 328–329): he grants the feeling of absolute certitude that accompanies inspiration was
"no deceiver" in the cases he cited, then warns this is not a rule without exception —
VERBATIM: **"often this feeling deceives us without being any the less vivid."** We only
find out, he says, when we try to build the demonstration; he especially noticed false
illuminations arriving in a "semi-hypnagogic state" (half-asleep). This is the direct
receipt that an "aha" is not self-certifying.

## 3. The aesthetic sieve (the selection theory)

Poincaré's mechanism for *why only good combinations surface*: the unconscious forms huge
numbers of combinations, but only those that affect **"our emotional sensibility"** cross
into consciousness. PARAPHRASE (Halsted, pp. 330–332): the relevant sensibility is the
feeling of mathematical beauty, harmony of numbers and forms, geometric elegance — "a true
esthetic feeling that all real mathematicians know." VERBATIM (p. 332): **"it is this
special esthetic sensibility which plays the role of the delicate sieve."** And the useful
combinations are "precisely the most beautiful" (VERBATIM, p. 331).

**The honesty check built into his own theory** (this is why it is not mysticism):
PARAPHRASE (Halsted, p. 332) — when a false illumination fails verification, "we almost
always notice that this false idea, had it been true, would have gratified our natural
feeling for mathematical elegance." I.e. beauty selects *candidates*; it does **not**
certify truth — a false idea can be just as beautiful. That distinction is the whole
transplantable lesson of §3.

**The hooked-atoms mechanism** (why preparation is the precondition, not optional).
PARAPHRASE (Halsted, pp. 333–334): Poincaré pictures the mind's elements as "the hooked
atoms of Epicurus." In complete rest they hang motionless on the wall; the *preliminary
conscious work* unhooks certain atoms and sets them in motion, and — because "our will did
not choose them at random" — the atoms freely dancing afterward are precisely the ones
relevant to the problem. So incubation recombines *only what deliberate effort first
mobilised*. No immersion, nothing to incubate.

## 4. "It is by logic that we prove, but by intuition that we discover"

VERBATIM (Poincaré): **"It is by logic that we prove, but by intuition that we discover."**
French: *"C'est par la logique qu'on démontre, c'est par l'intuition qu'on invente."*
**Provenance pinned:** *Science and Method* (*Science et Méthode*, 1908), Book II ("Mathematical
Reasoning"), Ch. 2 "Mathematical Definitions and Education," p. 129 in the Francis Maitland
translation (1914). It is **not** from *The Value of Science* — a common misattribution.
Continues: "To know how to criticize is good, to know how to create is better."

## 5. Hadamard's four-stage model — and the correct lineage

Hadamard, *An Essay on the Psychology of Invention in the Mathematical Field* (Princeton,
1945), systematised Poincaré's account into a four-stage cycle. PARAPHRASE (widely and
consistently attested; see MacTutor, Wikipedia, 1996 Johnson-Laird preface): **Preparation**
(conscious full-effort attack, usually appearing fruitless) → **Incubation** (the conscious
mind turns elsewhere while unconscious work proceeds) → **Illumination** (the solution
surfaces suddenly) → **Verification** (conscious work confirms the result, deduces
consequences, and makes it precise). Hadamard stresses the fourth stage is not merely
writing-up: it is needed for verification and for *precising* the result and its
consequences.

**Attribution hygiene — the lineage in order:**
1. **Helmholtz** (1891 speech) informally noted preparation → a pause → the idea arriving
   unbidden — PARAPHRASE.
2. **Poincaré** (1908 lecture) supplied the vivid first-person account *and* argued for the
   necessity of the closing conscious/verification stage — VERBATIM basis above.
3. **Graham Wallas**, *The Art of Thought* (1926), **named and formalised** the four stages
   — preparation, incubation, illumination, verification — explicitly building on Poincaré's
   "sudden illumination." This is the formalisation, and it is **pre-Hadamard**.
   PARAPHRASE (confirmed).
4. **Hadamard** (1945) applied the model systematically to mathematics and to a survey of
   mathematicians, crediting Poincaré's "masterly lecture" (VERBATIM fragment, MacTutor).

So the four-stage model is **not original to Hadamard**, and crediting it to him alone (or
to Poincaré alone) is the standard folklore error. Wallas is the formaliser; Poincaré the
primary witness; Hadamard the mathematician who stress-tested it against practice.

## 6. The Einstein letter — thought as wordless signs (understanding over storage)

Einstein answered Hadamard's survey in a 1945 letter, printed as an appendix. It is the
sharpest exhibit that the mathematical *workspace is not linguistic storage*. VERBATIM
(Einstein to Hadamard): **"The words or the language… do not seem to play any role in my
mechanism of thought."** The elements are instead **"certain signs and more or less clear
images"** that can be **"'voluntarily' reproduced and combined"** — and, in his case, "of
visual and some of muscular type." VERBATIM: **"combinatory play seems to be the essential
feature in productive thought."** Words are sought **"only in a secondary stage"** once the
associative play is established. Direct parallel to the plugin's understanding-over-storage
doctrine: the reasoning happens in a non-verbal combinatory workspace; language is the
output encoding, not the medium of thought.

## 7. Hadamard on his own practice, wordless thought, and error

PARAPHRASE (Hadamard, *Psychology of Invention*, consistently attested): Hadamard reports
that he himself thinks not in words but in vague mental images, and that most mathematicians
he surveyed said the same — words are avoided during the actual inventive work. He invokes
William James's **"fringe consciousness"** (the penumbra of half-attended elements around
the focus of attention) as what makes this introspection possible and as where incubated
material hovers before surfacing. On error: consistent with Poincaré, Hadamard treats
illumination as fallible and the fourth (verification) stage as non-negotiable — the
inspiration gives a *point of departure*, not a certified result. One of the few clean
direct quotes obtainable this session, VERBATIM (Hadamard, via MacTutor): **"We are rather
servants than masters in Mathematics."** — his gloss on how little of invention is under
conscious command.

## 8. Folklore flags (things NOT to repeat as fact)

- **"The four-stage model is Hadamard's."** WRONG. **Graham Wallas** named and formalised
  the four stages in *The Art of Thought* (1926), building on Poincaré (1908); Helmholtz
  gestured at three of them earlier. Hadamard (1945) applied and popularised the model in
  mathematics. Correct credit: **Poincaré (witness) → Wallas (formaliser) → Hadamard
  (applier).**
- **"The logic/intuition line is from *The Value of Science*."** Commonly cited that way;
  it is from ***Science and Method* (1908), Book II, Ch. 2**, Maitland trans. p. 129. Pin
  the right book.
- **Coffee-cup causation.** The black-coffee night is often retold as "coffee causes
  breakthroughs." Poincaré himself explicitly downgrades this: he calls the physical
  excitant merely the trigger of an abnormal cerebral activity and says such nights happen
  *without* any excitant (PARAPHRASE, Halsted p. 335). The coffee is incidental; the prior
  fifteen days of work are the cause.
- **"Incubation is passive rest."** FALSE per the primary source: Poincaré's Rule 2 makes
  incubation *fruitless* unless bracketed by real conscious work before and after. "Take a
  break and the answer comes" omits the load-bearing precondition.
- **"Illumination = truth."** Directly contradicted by Poincaré: the vivid feeling of
  certitude **"often… deceives us,"** especially in half-asleep states. Any account that
  treats the flash as self-certifying is folklore, not Poincaré.
- **Place-name/date micro-errors.** The bus episode is at **Coutances** (not "Constances");
  the essay is the **1908** lecture, printed in *Science et Méthode* (1908), Englished by
  Halsted in *The Monist* (1910) and again in *The Foundations of Science* (1913). One
  essay, several printings — don't multiply them into different works.

## 9. Transferable moves (mapped honestly onto the Euler Method)

Honest framing first: **the mechanism does not transplant, but the protocol does.** A
stateless AI session has no sleeping unconscious doing biological recombination between
turns — the literal "incubation" is the part that is *not* available. What transplants is
the *bracketing discipline* Poincaré proved was the actual source of value: full immersion
before, mandatory verification after, and a serial context-switch in between. That middle
step maps precisely onto Euler's documented habit of never abandoning a topic and returning
to it "relentlessly" (Weil) — so incubation, stripped of its neurology, **is** Euler's
serial-return habit. Flag that convergence: two independent primary records, same move.

1. **Earn the incubation — immerse fully first.** Poincaré: sudden inspirations "never
   happen except after some days of voluntary effort which has appeared absolutely
   fruitless" (Rule 2). *Euler Method map:* this is "compute FIRST and in appetite-sized
   quantities" and dwell on the table before conjecturing. **Corollary rule:** do not park
   a problem hoping for insight until you have genuinely exhausted a full-effort pass —
   there is nothing to incubate otherwise (the hooked-atoms argument, §3).

2. **Park with state written down, then work a different problem; return serially.** Since
   an AI session cannot incubate biologically, reproduce the *function* of incubation:
   write the full current state (what was tried, what's stuck, the candidate framings) into
   the Ledger/notes, switch to a genuinely different problem, and come back with a fresh
   pass. *Euler Method map:* exactly Euler's serial-return discipline (Weil: "never abandons
   a research topic… returns to it, relentlessly"). The written state is what makes the
   return possible across a context boundary.

3. **Log every "aha" as Conjectured, never Demonstrated — verification is a separate,
   mandatory step.** Poincaré: the feeling of certitude "often deceives us," found out only
   "when we seek to put on foot the demonstration"; and even in the true cases he still
   verified "for conscience' sake." *Euler Method map:* this is the Ledger, verbatim in
   spirit — an illumination enters as **Conjectured**, and only an independent verification
   pass can move it toward **Demonstrated**. The omnibus paragraph is the perfect exhibit:
   perfect certainty → deliberate later verification.

4. **Use elegance to choose where to look — but never as proof.** Poincaré's aesthetic
   sieve selects candidate combinations, yet a *false* idea "had it been true, would have
   gratified our natural feeling for mathematical elegance." *Euler Method map:* Euler's
   drive for "more natural, simpler, more direct" results is a real heuristic for *ranking
   what to try first* — but §3's honesty check forbids upgrading "this is beautiful" to
   "this is true." Beauty is a search prior; verification is the warrant.

5. **Keep the workspace non-verbal; use language as the output buffer.** Einstein: thought
   is "signs and more or less clear images," words sought "only in a secondary stage." This
   converges with Euler's blindness result in Dossier II — the paper was an output buffer,
   not the workspace. *Euler Method map:* reason in the structure (tables, invariants,
   relations), and treat prose/notation as the encoding written *after* the combinatory
   work — understanding over storage, applied to a session's own process.

6. **Treat interruption of a half-immersed problem as costly.** The four-stage model implies
   incubation only pays if preparation was completed; an interruption that aborts the
   full-effort pass leaves nothing mobilised to recombine (hooked-atoms, §3). *Euler Method
   map:* a practical scheduling rule — finish the immersion pass before parking, or the
   "return serially" move (Move 2) buys nothing. Park at a *saturated* point, not a random
   one.

**Net:** Poincaré and Hadamard supply, from two first-rank mathematicians' own testimony,
the discipline the Euler Method already runs on other grounds — immerse hard, park with
state, return serially, and never let a confident flash skip the Ledger. The incubation
literature's unique contribution to the plugin is the *proof that the flash is fallible*
and the *proof that immersion is its non-negotiable precondition* — both stated by the man
who lived them.
