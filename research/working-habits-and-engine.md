**Scope note on sources.** The two foundational eyewitness documents were read in full: Nicolas Fuss's *Lobrede/Éloge* (St. Petersburg Academy, 23 October 1783; Fuss was Euler's live-in assistant for his last ~10 years) in John S.D. ## 1. Eyewitness accounts of how he worked

**Work as a compulsive need — VERBATIM (Fuss, 1783):**
> "What misfortune for a man to who work had become a need, for which his spirit always on edge with some new discovery, finds itself all of a sudden unable to pursue his works."

**Ability to drop and resume deep work at will — VERBATIM (Fuss):**
> "Nothing was equal however; to his ability to drop his calculations and deep thoughts without the slightest hint of displeasure and after having involved himself in the frivolity of ordinary conversation take back up where he left off."

**Child on knee, cat on back — VERBATIM (Thiébault, via Gautschi):**
> "A child on the knees, a cat on his back, that's how he wrote his immortal works," recounts Dieudonné Thiébault.
(Gautschi, *SIAM Review* 50(1), 2008, p. 30. Original in Thiébault, *Mes souvenirs de vingt ans de séjour à Berlin*, 1804 — the exact French sentence was not independently located; treat wording as Gautschi's rendering.) MacTutor paraphrases the same tradition: "Euler claimed that he made some of his greatest mathematical discoveries while holding a baby in his arms with other children playing round his feet" — **PARAPHRASE**.

**Prodigious memory / Aeneid — VERBATIM (Fuss):**
> "It was the effect of a prodigious memory which loses nothing from a lecture he has heard, and Euler was capable of reciting the Aeneid without interruption from beginning to end and to indicated the beginning and ending line of each page of the edition, and this was also something which he retained in his older years."

Condorcet independently — **VERBATIM (French original):** "il n'avait pas lu les poètes modernes, et savait par cœur l'Énéide. Cependant M. Euler ne perdait pas de vue les mathématiques, même lorsqu'il récitait les vers de Virgile... on trouve dans ses ouvrages un savant mémoire sur une question de mécanique, dont il racontait qu'un vers de l'Énéide lui avait donné la première idée." (A memoir in mechanics sparked by a verse of Virgil.)

**Mental calculation, insomnia powers table — VERBATIM (Fuss, footnote; he was present):**
> "He gave lessons in algebra and geometry to his grandsons. He often proposes numbers for which the extractions of their roots were powers. He calculated them in his head, and when unable to sleep because of insomnia he calculated the first six powers of all the numbers under twenty and recited them to us to our great astonishment a few days later."

Condorcet's version — **VERBATIM (French):** "on l'a vu, dans l'intention d'exercer son petit-fils aux extractions des racines, se former la table des six premières puissances de tous les nombres, depuis 1 jusqu'à 100, et la conserver exactement dans sa mémoire." *Discrepancy flag:* Fuss (eyewitness) says first six powers of numbers under 20 in one sleepless night; Condorcet says a memorized table for 1–100. Gautschi notes the divergence explicitly (p. 30).

**The students' dispute — VERBATIM (Condorcet, French original; the folklore "50th decimal place" version is garbled):**
> "Deux de ses disciples avaient calculé jusqu'au dix-septième terme une série convergente assez compliquée ; leurs résultats, quoique formés d'après un calcul écrit, différaient d'une unité au cinquantième chiffre : ils firent part de cette dispute à leur maître ; M. Euler refit le calcul entier dans sa tête, et sa décision se trouva conforme à la vérité."
(Two students summed a complicated convergent series to the 17th term; their written results differed by one unit in the 50th figure; Euler redid the entire calculation *in his head* and settled it.)

**d'Alembert's astonishment — VERBATIM (Condorcet, Glaus):** "When Mr. d'Alembert saw Euler in Berlin he was astonished by the prodigious memory that Mr. Euler possessed and finally the ease with which he calculated in his head."

**"A paper between the first and second calls to dinner" — LEGEND-UNVERIFIED.** Not in Fuss, not in Condorcet (both full texts checked), not in Rouse Ball. Earliest traced form: E.T. Bell, *Men of Mathematics* (1937). Bell is notoriously embellishment-prone. The *documented* basis for "could work anywhere, anytime" is Thiébault's child/cat line plus Gautschi's summary: "an ease in concentrating on mental work irrespective of any hustle and bustle going on around him" (**PARAPHRASE** of primary testimony, Gautschi p. 30).

## 2. Blindness and productivity

**Timeline (PARAPHRASE, cross-confirmed Gautschi + MacTutor):** 1735 near-fatal fever; 1738 loss of (most) sight in right eye — Euler blamed overstrain from cartographic work; Calinger disputes the causal link. By 1740 Euler wrote he had "lost an eye and [the other] currently may be in the same danger." Cataract in the left (good) eye worsened after the 1766 return to St. Petersburg; 1771 cataract operation succeeded but an abscess "soon destroyed Euler's vision almost entirely" (Gautschi p. 9). Fuss: he retained "the faculty of distinguishing large characters traced with chalk on a slate."

**The "fewer distractions" quote — LEGEND-UNVERIFIED as a first-person quip.** "Now I will have fewer distractions" is first documented in Howard Eves, *In Mathematical Circles* (1969) — 186 years after the fact. **But it has a genuine 1783 ancestor, in the third person — VERBATIM (Condorcet, French):**
> "ses fils, ses élèves, copiaient ses calculs, écrivaient sous sa dictée le reste de ses mémoires ; et si on en juge par leur nombre... on pourrait croire que l'absence encore plus absolue de toute distraction, et la nouvelle énergie que ce recueillement forcé donnait à toutes ses facultés, lui ont fait plus gagner, que l'affaiblissement de sa vue n'a pu lui faire perdre de facilité et de moyens pour le travail."
("...one might believe that the still-more-absolute absence of all distraction, and the new energy this forced concentration gave all his faculties, made him gain more than the weakening of his sight could make him lose.") Fuss says the same: "his prodigious and astonishing memory, enforced by the concentration of all of its forces disengaged from external distraction supplemented what might have been a reason to end the literary career of such a great man." The *observation* is contemporary and doubly attested; the snappy first-person *quote* is a 20th-century compression.

**Output while blind — PARAPHRASE, well-attested:** "about half of his scientific output was published, or originated, during this second St. Petersburg period" (Gautschi p. 9); MacTutor: "he produced almost half his total works despite the total blindness." In 1775 he produced on average one mathematical paper per week. Scribes: his son Johann Albrecht, Fuss (daily for the last decade), Golovin, Krafft, Lexell — MacTutor stresses they "were not mere secretaries; he discussed the general scheme of the works with them, and they developed his ideas, calculating tables, and sometimes compiled examples."

**The *Algebra* dictated to a tailor — VERBATIM (Fuss):** "A young tailor who he had brought from Berlin as a servant and who possessed no talents for mathematics, became the scribe to whom Euler dictated his Elements of Algebra." Gautschi adds (from the work's preface) that the apprentice "was fairly good at computing, but beyond that did not have the slightest notion about mathematics," yet by the end "understood everything perfectly well and was able to solve algebraic problems posed to him with great ease."

**Where the method lived — VERBATIM (Condorcet, French), the key passage:**
> "on voit qu'elles ne sont pas le fruit d'un calcul tracé sur le papier, et que, produites tout entières dans sa tête, elles y ont été créées par une imagination également puissante et active."
(Of his great analytic formulas: "they are not the fruit of a calculation traced on paper; produced entire in his head, they were created there by an imagination equally powerful and active.") And Fuss on the Second Lunar Theory (E418, 1772), computed blind, house burned, notes lost: "Deprived of his sight and obligated to make most of the immense calculations by the sole intermediary of his memory and imagination... Mr. Euler was still in a state to compose a work that of its self would immortalize him." Footnote: the lunar-theory notes were destroyed in the 1771 fire and his son "was obligated to redo all the lunar theory and to re-calculate everything for a second time."

## 3. Volume and appetite

- **Eneström index: 866 numbered works** — standard count (Eneström 1910). **VERIFIED.**
- **Opera Omnia: 74 quarto volumes as of 2008 "(with two more to come)"; Series I = 30 vols (math), II = 31 (mechanics/astronomy), III = 12 (physics/misc), IVA = correspondence, plus "about seven volumes of notebooks and diaries still await editing"** — VERBATIM/PARAPHRASE from Gautschi p. 3 and Aycock (arXiv:2505.23286).
- **Share of the century — VERBATIM (Truesdell):** "Approximately one-third of the entire corpus of research on mathematics and mathematical physics and engineering mechanics published in the last three-quarters of the eighteenth century is by him." (Truesdell, "Leonard Euler, Supreme Geometer," 1972; reprinted in *An Idiot's Fugitive Essays on Science*, 1984.)
- **Backlog — VERBATIM (Condorcet, 1783):** "more than thirty works published outside and the more than seven hundred Mémoires of which two hundred were deposited before his death and are destined to enrich the collection that the Academy publishes."
- **The Orlov promise — VERBATIM (Fuss):** "Mr. Euler had told Count Orlov on more than one occasion that he would provide the Academy with sufficient mémoires to fill the Acta for at least twenty years after his death. He was a man to keep his promise." Fuss adds the dictation ledger: "In the space of seven years... Mr. Golovine worked on more than 70 mémoires and there were over 250 others for which I had done the calculations."
- **Actual posthumous run — PARAPHRASE (Gautschi p. 10):** "A stream of memoirs, however, all queued up at the presses of the Academy, were still to be published for nearly fifty years after Euler's death." The promise was *under*-kept by a factor of ~2.5.
- **"He wrote faster than they could print" — LEGEND-flavored but grounded:** no 18th-century source for the sentence; a popularizer's compression of the two documented facts above. Phrasing LEGEND-UNVERIFIED; substance verified.

## 4. Motivation evidence

**The thesis sentence, from Condorcet — VERBATIM (French):**
> "On a dit que, pour les hommes d'un grand talent, le plaisir du travail en était une récompense plus douce encore que la gloire ; si cette vérité avait besoin d'être prouvée par des exemples, celui de M. Euler ne permettrait plus d'en douter."
("It has been said that for men of great talent, the pleasure of the work is a reward sweeter still than glory; if this truth needed proof by example, Euler's would no longer permit doubt of it.")

**Study as exclusive passion — VERBATIM (Condorcet, French):** "l'étude était pour M. Euler une passion exclusive."

**Joy at new truths, and annoyance at those who don't feel it — VERBATIM (Fuss, footnote; firsthand):**
> "...the pleasure that a mathematician has the advantage of experiencing perhaps more than others. Mr. Euler always enjoyed this type of susceptibility and he would wish that everyone experience the same. He would become seriously upset with the attitude of indifference that my modest temperament made to assume when I told him the solution to a problem or a proof of a theorem that I was able to find."

**Redoing his own solved problems for elegance alone — VERBATIM (Condorcet, Glaus):** "One sees Euler using his time to remake and illuminate his works, and to resolve problems that were already resolved to which remained only the necessity of continued elegance and improved method which he did with the same passion, the same constancy which he would have put to use in the pursuit of a new truth."

**No priority jealousy — PARAPHRASE (Gautschi p. 30):** "Feelings of rancor, due to either priority issues or unfair criticism, were totally foreign to Euler." Documented cases: (a) the Euler–Maclaurin formula circulating as "Maclaurin's" without objection — Euler to Stirling, ~1738, **VERBATIM (MacTutor):** "I have very little desire for anything to be detracted from the fame of the celebrated Mr Maclaurin since he probably came upon the same theorem for summing series before me, and consequently deserves to be named as its first discoverer"; (b) putting his own hydrodynamics on hold "so that his friend Daniel Bernoulli... could complete and publish his own Hydrodynamics first" (Gautschi p. 30).

**The Lagrange / calculus-of-variations story — accurate version.** Lagrange, aged 19, wrote to Euler on 12 August 1755 with the purely analytic δ-method; Euler replied 6 September 1755 "saying how impressed he was with Lagrange's new ideas" (MacTutor, PARAPHRASE). The strong claim — **VERBATIM (Rouse Ball):** "Euler recognized the generality of the method adopted, and its superiority to that used by himself; and with rare courtesy he withheld a paper he had previously written, which covered some of the same ground, in order that the young Italian might have time to complete his work, and claim the undisputed invention of the new calculus." **Caveat flag:** "this chivalric view has been disputed" (D. Galletto, 1989, via Wikipedia). Fact-check anchors: Euler did coin the name "calculus of variations," did credit Lagrange, and his own analytic treatments appeared only after Lagrange's 1762 publication. Verdict: generosity documented; the specific "withheld the paper" mechanism plausible but contested — **PARAPHRASE-DISPUTED**.

**Goldbach correspondence tone — VERBATIM (Euler to Goldbach, 30 June 1742, standard translation):** "That ... every even integer is a sum of two primes, I regard as a completely certain theorem, although I cannot prove it." The 35-year, ~196-letter correspondence is the canonical exhibit of Euler doing number theory purely for delight — the field had negligible career value; Gautschi: "the mathematical community of the time (Lagrange excluded!) was rather indifferent to problems of this kind" (p. 15).

**Pedagogical drive:** *Letters to a German Princess* (1760–62): Condorcet devotes a paragraph to its "singular clarity" and mass readership — "those who have not studied Mathematics, are astonished and flattered to be able to understand a work by Euler" (VERBATIM, Glaus). Condorcet on his expository ethic — VERBATIM (Glaus): "he much preferred the education of his students than the small satisfaction derived from astonishment; he never believed that he had truly done enough for Science if he did not feel that that he had added new truths to enrich it and the exposure of the simplicity of the idea which lead him there."

**Counterweight (for honesty):** Condorcet also records the contemporary criticism — VERBATIM (Glaus): "At times Mr. Euler appeared only to enjoy the pleasures of calculation; and to look at the point in Mechanics or Physics which he examined only as an occasion to exercise his genius and to surrender to his dominant passion." Even the *criticism* of Euler is that his intrinsic drive was too strong.

## 6. Historians on why the method worked

**André Weil — VERBATIM (as quoted in Gautschi p. 31, from Weil, "L'œuvre arithmétique d'Euler," 1983, pp. 132–133):**
> "...what at first is striking about Euler is his extraordinary quickness in catching hold of any suggestion, wherever it came from... There is not one of these suggestions which in Euler's hands has not become the point of departure of an impressive series of researches... Another thing, not less striking, is that Euler never abandons a research topic, once it has excited his curiosity; on the contrary, he returns to it, relentlessly, in order to deepen and broaden it on each revisit. Even if all problems related to such a topic seem to be resolved, he never ceases until the end of his life to find proofs that are 'more natural,' 'simpler,' 'more direct.'"
Concrete instance: partitions — from Naudé's 1740 letter through memoirs spanning ~20 years (E158 to E394; Gautschi p. 27).

**Hermann Hankel (1869) — VERBATIM (German, quoted in A. Aycock, "Eulers Horizonte," arXiv:2505.23286):**
> "Er [Euler] war eine wesentlich concrete Natur, die sich mit wirklicher Liebe und Begeisterung dem Stoffe hingab und sich von ihm treiben liess. [...] Daher geht durch all seine Schriften ein warmer, lebendiger Zug; man liest zwischen den Zeilen überall begeisterte Freude über die Schönheit und wunderbare Tiefe, die ihm der Gegenstand offenbart... so lesen sich seine Schriften wie 'Novellen'."
("He gave himself to the material with real love and enthusiasm and let himself be driven by it... between the lines one reads everywhere enthusiastic joy at the beauty and wonderful depth the subject reveals to him... his writings read like novellas.")

**Gautschi on craftsmanship (PARAPHRASE, p. 31):** always strove for "utmost clarity and simplicity," revisited earlier work that fell short; "in his quest for discovery, he could be fearless, even reckless, but owing to his secure instinct, he rarely went astray."

**Writing-as-thinking / showing the full process:** Aycock (arXiv:2505.23286 — the most detailed modern study of Euler's *Arbeitsweise*) identifies as signature habits: exhaustive presentation of the train of thought and calculations; verifying the same theorem repeatedly by different routes ("Die mehrfache Bestätigung eines Theorems auf verschiedenen Wegen"); and — rarest of all — **publishing failed attempts in full** ("Mitteilung von Misserfolgen"). Condorcet corroborates: "he exposed with the greatest simplicity the path that he chose to follow, he brought to light the difficulties or detours" (VERBATIM, Glaus). Frobenius (quoted in Opera Omnia Ser. I Vol. 6 preface, via Aycock): "Euler fehlt nur eine Eigenschaft zu einem vollkommenen Genie: nämlich unverständlich zu sein" — "Euler lacked only one thing to be a perfect genius: being incomprehensible."

**The human-computer side:** Condorcet on the lunar theory — VERBATIM (Glaus): "one is no less astonished to see the lengths to which a great man of genius, animated exclusively by the desire to leave nothing to chance concerning important issues, can push the limits of patience and the obstinacy to work." The lunar theories fed navigational tables used "for over a century" (Gautschi p. 26).

**Chance favors the prepared mind — VERBATIM (Condorcet, Glaus):** "When Mr. Euler made mention that it was to chance that he owed such discovery, it was not as an attempt to diminish the importance, since one could easily see that chance favors the prepared mind of a person who possesses a vast body of knowledge coupled with a rare wisdom."

**On learning by self-struggle (Euler's autobiography, dictated 1767, via Fellmann/Aycock) — VERBATIM (German):** Johann Bernoulli refused private lessons but told him to work through books alone and bring stuck points on Saturday afternoons, "welches gewiss die beste Methode ist, um in den mathematischen Wissenschaften glückliche Progressen zu machen" ("which is certainly the best method to make happy progress in the mathematical sciences").

## 7. Verdict on the hypothesis

*"Euler had genuine intrinsic interest, so he had no incentive to stop himself from diving fully into problems — tables and tables, pages and pages by hand, staring at and thinking about the numbers."*

**Strongly supported, with one correction.** Supported: the pleasure-of-work-over-glory sentence (Condorcet), work-as-need (Fuss), joy at theorems and irritation at Fuss's indifference to them (Fuss, firsthand), zero priority-defense and active glory-donation (Maclaurin, D. Bernoulli, Lagrange episodes), re-solving already-solved problems purely for elegance "with the same passion" (Condorcet), returning to topics "once [they have] excited his curiosity... relentlessly" for decades (Weil), doing number theory for 35 years in private letters when the field had no market (Goldbach correspondence), and continuing at full rate through total blindness until literally the afternoon of his death (both eulogies). The contemporaneous *criticism* of Euler was that he enjoyed calculation too much.

**The correction:** the "pages and pages by hand" image is half wrong, and the blindness episode is the proof. Condorcet states outright that the great formulas "are not the fruit of a calculation traced on paper" but were "produced entire in his head"; the students' 17-term series was re-run mentally; the powers tables were *memorized* artifacts; the Second Lunar Theory was computed "by the sole intermediary of his memory and imagination." The paper (and later the scribes and the big slate) was an output buffer, not the workspace — which is exactly why removing his eyes removed nothing essential and, per both 1783 eulogists, arguably *added* concentration. The tables-and-tables appetite was real, but it was fed by an internal representation so robust that the 1766–1783 blind period yielded roughly half of an 866-item corpus.

**Folklore firewall (things NOT to repeat as fact):** the Diderot proof-of-God scene (apocryphal); "Now I will have fewer distractions" as a first-person quip (Eves 1969); "wrote a paper between the first and second calls to dinner" (Bell 1937); Laplace's "Read Euler" (Libri hearsay, 1846); "settled the dispute at the 50th decimal place" (actually one unit in the 50th figure of a 17-term series sum, per Condorcet's French); and note that even Gautschi's SIAM article misattributes Arago's "as men breathe" line to Condorcet.
