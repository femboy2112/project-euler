## Thread 1 — The "experimental mathematics" lineage: Euler as founder figure

**1.1 Zeilberger names Euler the arch experimental mathematician.** VERBATIM (paper abstract): "One of the greatest experimental mathematicians of all time was also one of the greatest mathematicians of all time, the great Leonhard Euler. Usually he had an uncanny intuition on how many 'special cases' one needs before one can formulate a plausible conjecture, but one time he was 'almost fooled', only to find out that his conjecture was premature. In 1990, George Andrews found a way to 'correct' Euler..." — Ekhad & Zeilberger, "On Euler's 'Misleading Induction', Andrews' 'Fix', and How to Fully Automate them," 2013. https://arxiv.org/abs/1304.1226

**1.2 Borwein–Bailey's canonical definition of the loop.** VERBATIM (quoted identically in two independent sources read): "Experimental mathematics is the methodology of doing mathematics that includes the use of computations for: 1. Gaining insight and intuition. 2. Discovering new patterns and relationships. 3. Using graphical displays to suggest underlying mathematical principles. 4. Testing and especially falsifying conjectures. 5. Exploring a possible result to see if it is worth formal proof. 6. Suggesting approaches for formal proof. 7. Replacing lengthy hand derivations with computer-based derivations. 8. Confirming analytically derived results." — Borwein & Bailey, *Mathematics by Experiment: Plausible Reasoning in the 21st Century* (2003), as quoted in (a) Shallit's review, *Notices AMS* 52:8 (2005) — https://cs.uwaterloo.ca/~shallit/ams-rev-borwein.pdf, and (b) Nguyen, *Mathematics by Experiment* sample chapters — https://users.rowan.edu/~nguyen/experimentalmath/MathematicsbyExperimentTextbookSampleChapters.pdf. The book's subtitle is a deliberate homage to Pólya — whose exemplary figure is Euler.

**1.3 The line runs literally through Euler's problems.** PARAPHRASE: Bailey, Borwein & Girgensohn, "Experimental evaluation of Euler sums," *Experimental Mathematics* 3:1 (1994) — the flagship modern experimental-math paper is a direct continuation of Euler's Goldbach-correspondence-era double-sum program.

**1.4 Pólya as the bridge.** (See Dossier I §5 for the full "observation, daring guess, and shrewd verification" passage — reproduced in Nguyen, read this session.) **The master text for "Euler is imitable because he shows his process."**

**1.5 The journal and its manifesto.** PARAPHRASE (PDF 403'd; content via search snippets): *Experimental Mathematics* (founded 1992); Epstein & Levy, "Experimentation and Proof in Mathematics," *Notices AMS* 42 (1995): "Experiment has always been, and increasingly is, an important method of mathematical discovery"; the journal publishes significant conjectures as such. Exact wording beyond quoted phrases UNVERIFIED.

**1.6 Modern hygiene notes.** VERBATIM (Shallit review): "mindless computation can be counterproductive… Time used thinking—away from the computer—is often time well spent"; "naive computation can lead to incorrect conjectures," citing the books' section "**High Precision Fraud**" (a sum agreeing with 1/81 to hundreds of digits yet unequal).

**1.7 Pedagogical framing.** VERBATIM (epigraph in Nguyen, attributed): "Euler's work reminds us of what we should strive for in the teaching of our students and ourselves — to observe, to experiment, to search for patterns, and above all, to rejoice not just in the final rigor, but in that wondrous process of discovery that must always precede it." — Robert M. Young, *Excursions in Calculus* (MAA, 1992).

## Thread 2 — André Weil's assessment

**2.1 The concluding-section portrait (the core "engine" text).** VERBATIM (Weil, *Number Theory: An Approach Through History from Hammurapi to Legendre*, 1984, ch. III; quoted in full in A. Papadopoulos, "Looking backward: From Euler to Riemann," p. 4 — https://arxiv.org/pdf/1710.03982):

> "[...] Hardly less striking is the fact that Euler never abandoned a problem after it has once aroused his insatiable curiosity. Other mathematicians, Hilbert for instance, have had their lives neatly divided into periods, each one devoted to a separate topic. Not so Euler. All his life, even after the loss of his eyesight, he seems to have carried in his head the whole of the mathematics of his day, both pure and applied. Once he has taken up a question, not only did he come back to it again and again, little caring if at times he was merely repeating himself, but also he loved to cast his net wider and wider with never failing enthusiasm, always expecting to uncover more and more mysteries, more and more 'herrliche proprietates' lurching just around the next corner. Nor did it matter to him whether he or another made the discovery. 'Penitus obstupui', he writes ('I was quite flabbergasted': Eu.I-21.1 in E 506|1777, cf. his last letter to Lagrange, Eu.IV A-5.505|1775) on learning Lagrange's additions to his own work on elliptic integrals; after which he proceeds to improve upon Lagrange's achievement. Even when a problem seemed to have been solved to his own satisfaction (as happened with his first proof of Fermat's theorem a^p ≡ a mod p, or in 1749 with sums of two squares) he never rested in his search for better proofs, 'more natural' (Eu. I-2.510 in E 262|1755), 'easy' (Eu.1-3.504 in E 522|1772), 'direct' (Eu.I-2.363 in E 242|1751); and repeatedly he found them."

Papadopoulos on Weil's own method, VERBATIM: "In Weil's book, every piece of historical information is accompanied by a precise reference. Works that attain this level of scholarship are very rare."

**2.2 Undisputed leadership.** VERBATIM (standard citation Weil 1984): "No mathematician ever attained such a position of undisputed leadership in all branches of mathematics, pure and applied, as Euler did for the best part of the eighteenth century." (Page number unverified this session.)

**2.3 Quickness + relentless return.** VERBATIM (Weil 1983, trans. quoted in Gautschi p. 31): "…what at first is striking about Euler is his extraordinary quickness in catching hold of any suggestion, wherever it came from…. There is not one of these suggestions which in Euler's hands has not become the point of departure of an impressive series of researches…. Another thing, not less striking, is that Euler never abandons a research topic, once it has excited his curiosity; on the contrary, he returns to it, relentlessly, in order to deepen and broaden it on each revisit."

**2.4 Weil on Euler anticipating rigor through calculation (zeta functional equation).** VERBATIM/PARAPHRASE (Papadopoulos pp. 37–38): In E352 Euler wrote a formula from which the functional equation follows, "as pointed out by Weil"; Weil (from *Two lectures on number theory, past and present*, p. 10): "Euler proves the formula for every positive integer n (using the so-called Abel summation to give a meaning to the divergent series in the numerator of the left hand side), and conjectures its validity for all n." Riemann proved it 110 years later. Also: Weil considers (Number Theory p. 278) that after Euler, the subject was dead, and Riemann resurrected it.

**2.5 "Publishing everything including gropings."** UNVERIFIED as a verbatim Weil sentence ("nothing was beneath him" not found; treat as folklore/paraphrase). Documented near-equivalent from P.H. Fuss (1843 introduction to the correspondence, transl. Papadopoulos, p. 5): "the life of a scholar was, in some way, all reflected in that correspondence. We see there the great discoveries being prepared and gradually developed; no link and no transition is missing; the path which led to these discoveries is followed step by step, and we can draw there some information even in the errors committed by these great geniuses."

## Thread 3 — Genius = appetite: volume, stamina, love of calculation

**3.1 Truesdell's one-third estimate.** VERBATIM (standard wording): "Approximately one-third of the entire corpus of research on mathematics and mathematical physics and engineering mechanics published in the last three-quarters of the eighteenth century is by him." — Truesdell, "Leonard Euler, Supreme Geometer" (1972).

**3.2 Condorcet.** VERBATIM: "…il cessa de calculer et de vivre." Also (via Dunham's book): future mathematicians will be "guided and sustained by the genius of Euler" and "all mathematicians… are his disciples."

**3.5 Volume facts.** PARAPHRASE (Gautschi): Opera Omnia = 74 large volumes (+2 to come) plus ~9 of correspondence and ~7 of notebooks; about half his output originated in the blind period 1766–83; queued memoirs kept appearing "for nearly fifty years" after death. Rouse Ball via Derbyshire, VERBATIM: "there is for each and every fortnight in 47 years a separate effort of mathematical invention."

**3.6 The Kac "ordinary genius vs magician" frame applied to Euler.** **UNVERIFIED — no citable source found.** Kac's frame (*Enigmas of Chance*, 1985) is about Bethe vs Feynman; no historian was found applying it to Euler. The *documented* equivalents of "Euler's method is visible and imitable": Pólya's preface, Condorcet's "candid exposition," Weil 2.1, Fuss's "no link and no transition is missing." ## Thread 4 — Euler's own statements about method, and the Goldbach engine

**4.1 E256 — the manifesto.** (Full passage in Dossier I §2.) Key discipline clause, VERBATIM: "we should take great care not to accept as true such properties of the numbers which we have discovered by observation… Indeed, we should use such a discovery as an opportunity to investigate more exactly the properties discovered and to prove or disprove them; **in both cases we may learn something useful**."

**4.2 E175 — the documented experimental loop, in Euler's voice.** (Full quotes in Dossier I §1.) Chronology (Gautschi): the pentagonal expansion "conjectured as early as 1742 by numerical computation, and then labored on… for almost ten years to find a proof (in E244, a 'masterpiece' according to C.G.J. Jacobi)" — he *used* the unproven identity for a decade, with the label honest the whole time.

**4.3 First letter to Goldbach (Oct 13, 1729, on the gamma function).** VERBATIM (transl. OO IVA/4, quoted in Papadopoulos p. 36): "For up to now my method has the drawback that I cannot find what I want, but rather have to be content with wanting what I find." — Euler's own one-line description of exploratory research.

**4.4 The Basel-problem announcement tone.** VERBATIM (E41 introduction, transl. André Weil, *Number Theory* p. 261, quoted in Papadopoulos p. 37): "So much work has been done on the series ζ(n) that it seems hardly likely that anything new about them may still turn up… I too, in spite of repeated efforts, could achieve nothing more than approximate values for their sums… Now, however, quite unexpectedly, I have found an elegant formula for ζ(2), depending upon the quadrature of the circle."

**4.5 The 1729 postscript and the 50-year capture.** Timeline from F. Lemmermeyer, "Euler, Goldbach, and 'Fermat's Theorem'" (arXiv:1310.6605, read; translations by Martin Mattmüller from OO IVA/4):
- **Letter 2, Dec 1, 1729** (Goldbach, postscript): asks whether Euler knows Fermat's claim that all 2^(2^n)+1 are prime.
- **Letter 3, Jan 8, 1730** (Euler): "Euler is unable to do anything with Fermat's problem" — the documented initial indifference.
- **Letter 4, May 22, 1730**: Goldbach *teaches Euler modular arithmetic* ("explains how to compute with remainders") — the needling correspondent supplying tooling.
- **Letter 5, June 4, 1730** (Euler, five weeks later): proves 2^n+1 composite if n has an odd prime divisor, and VERBATIM: "Lately, reading Fermat's works, I came upon another rather elegant theorem stating that any number is the sum of four squares…" — capture complete.
- **Letter 7, June 25, 1730**: Euler reports computations (10⁴+1 divisible by 37; 3⁸+2⁸ by 17) — *computing back within weeks* is literal.
- **Letter 15, Nov 25, 1730**: "By studying prime divisors of numbers 2^p − 1, Euler discovered 'Fermat's Little Theorem'."
- **Letter 52, June 30, 1742**: Euler mentions 641 divides 2³²+1.
- Four-squares (Apr 12, 1749), VERBATIM: "I can almost prove that any number is a sum of four or fewer squares; indeed, what I am lacking is just one proposition, which does not appear to present any difficulty at first sight" — with honest gap-labels: proposition (2) "is the theorem on which the whole matter depends, and which I cannot yet prove." Lemmermeyer's framing, VERBATIM: "There are perhaps no better examples in Goldbach's correspondence with Euler for illuminating his role as a catalyst than the letters discussing various aspects of the Four-Squares Theorem."
- Scale: **196 letters, 1729–1764, 102 by Euler.** Papadopoulos (p. 5): "Goldbach had a tremendous influence on Euler, by being attentive to his progress, by the questions he asked him on number theory, and also by motivating him to read Diophantus and Fermat." P.H. Fuss (1843): "It is more than probable that if this intimate relationship between Euler and this scholar, a relationship that lasted 36 years without interruption, hadn't been there, then the science of numbers would have never attained the degree of perfection which it owes to the immortal discoveries of Euler."
- Second "correspondent ignites program" case (Gautschi p. 27): Philippe Naudé's 1740 partition question → "a series of memoirs, spanning a time interval of about 20 years… In this work, Euler almost single-handedly created the theory of partition."

**4.6 E212 part 2 on induction in series.** A specific methodological "induction" discussion there was **not located — UNVERIFIED**. The documented methodological texts are E256, E175, and the pentagonal papers.

## Thread 5 — Pedagogy as method

**5.1 Boyer's ICM assessment of the Introductio.** VERBATIM (Boyer, "The Foremost Textbook of Modern Times," *AMM* 58 (1951); quoted in Papadopoulos p. 12): "The most influential mathematics textbooks of ancient times (or, for that matter, of all times) is easily named. The *Elements* of Euclid… [...] over these well known textbooks there towers another, a work which appeared in the very middle of the great textbook age and to which virtually all later writers admitted indebtedness. This was the *Introductio in analysin infinitorum* of Euler, published in two volumes in 1748. Here in effect Euler accomplished for analysis what Euclid and al-Khwārizmī had done for synthetic geometry and elementary algebra, respectively."

**5.2 Weil's pedagogy verdict.** VERBATIM (Blanton's translator's introduction, 1988; quoted in Papadopoulos p. 12): "In October, 1979, Professor André Weil spoke at the University of Rochester on the life and work of Leonhard Euler. One of his remarks was to the effect that he was trying to convince the mathematical community that students of mathematics would profit much more from a study of Euler's *Introductio in analysin infinitorum*, rather than the available modern textbooks."

**5.3 Laplace, with provenance.** VERBATIM: "Lisez Euler, lisez Euler, c'est notre maître à tous." Reported by Guglielmo Libri, *Journal des Savants*, January 1846, p. 51, claiming to have heard it from Laplace's own mouth. Secondhand, 19 years after Laplace's death — cite as "reported by Libri."

**5.4 Gauss.** VERBATIM (common translation): "The study of Euler's works will remain the best school for the different fields of mathematics and nothing else can replace it." Secondhand (Fellmann 1995 p. 122; Dunnington 1955 p. 361); original German locus unverified this session.

**5.5 Textbooks at every level, and the research feedback loop.** PARAPHRASE with verbatim kernels (Gautschi): Rouse Ball on E212: "this is the first textbook on the differential calculus which has any claim to be both complete and accurate." The Introductio's ch. 16 contains the systematic exposition of his own partition research — exposition and research in one organism. Turgot proposed to Louis XVI that marine/artillery schools require Euler's treatises; the King paid 1,000 rubles for reprint rights and Catherine II added 3,000. His two "best-sellers" (*Letters to a German Princess*, the *Algebra*) came from the blind period. A *stronger causal* claim ("organizing knowledge generated new results") in those words was **not found — treat as synthesis**, supportable by the Introductio/partitions case and Weil 2.1 (re-exposition as research).

**5.6 Generosity as method-adjacent trait.** PARAPHRASE (Gautschi p. 30): Euler–Maclaurin circulating as "Maclaurin's" without complaint; hydrodynamics on hold for Daniel Bernoulli; "Sharing ideas with others and letting others take part in the process of discovery is another noble trait of Euler."

## Thread 6 — Failure tolerance: conjectured freely, sometimes wrong, labels honest

**6.1 The 36 officers / Graeco-Latin squares conjecture (wrong).** VERIFIED CASE. Euler, E530 (1782), VERBATIM (trans. in Dey's keynote): "After we have put a lot of thought into finding a solution, we have to admit that such an arrangement is impossible, though we can't give a rigorous demonstration of this." He conjectured no Graeco-Latin squares of order n ≡ 2 (mod 4). Tarry (1901) confirmed n = 6; Bose & Shrikhande (1959) and Parker (order 10) then Bose–Shrikhande–Parker (1960) disproved it for all n ≡ 2 (mod 4), n > 6. Front page of the *New York Times*, April 26, 1959; the trio dubbed the "Euler Spoilers." Note: Euler was right about n = 6 — wrong only in the generalization, and he had labeled it as unproven.

**6.2 Euler's sum of powers conjecture (wrong).** VERIFIED CASE. Conjectured 1769; disproved by Lander & Parkin, *Bull. AMS* 72 (1966) — a two-sentence paper reporting a CDC 6600 search: 27⁵ + 84⁵ + 110⁵ + 133⁵ = 144⁵. (Elkies 1988 for n=4; Frye's minimal 95800⁴+217519⁴+414560⁴=422481⁴.) Poetic symmetry: Euler's conjecture fell to exactly the machine-assisted experimental search he pioneered by hand.

**6.3 Euler as executioner of Fermat's conjecture.** VERIFIED: the very conjecture Goldbach pointed him at in 1729 is one Euler *disproved* by computation (641 × 6700417). The method is symmetric: it kills conjectures — including his own and Fermat's — as readily as it makes them.

**6.4 Euler's own "misleading induction."** VERIFIED via Zeilberger (1.1) and Sandifer HEDI-2005-08 (Dossier III §3): the E326 *exemplum*. Pairs with E256's warning: "mere induction led to error… in both cases we may learn something useful."

**6.5 Odd perfect numbers — premise correction.** ⚠️ No wrong Euler conjecture here. Euler *proved* the Euclid–Euler theorem and a structure theorem for hypothetical odd perfect numbers. Do not attribute an odd-perfect error to Euler.

**6.6 Fermat's Last Theorem n=3.** PLAUSIBLE/standard (not primary-verified): Euler announced a proof to Goldbach Aug 4, 1753; his published argument (Algebra, 1770) has a known gap (implicit unique factorization in Z[√−3]) — a real, documented error in a real proof, later repairable.

**6.7 Divergent series.** Gautschi: "in his quest for discovery, he could be fearless, even reckless, but owing to his secure instinct, he rarely went astray when his argumentation became hasty." Weil's zeta commentary shows the "reckless" manipulations produced the functional equation a century early. The modern hygiene echo is Borwein–Bailey's "High Precision Fraud" (1.6).

## Cross-thread synthesis hooks (each anchored above)

1. **The loop is documented in Euler's own prose**: compute massively → spot pattern → conjecture with explicit epistemic label → verify on more data → prove later, sometimes a decade later, sometimes never, sometimes get killed.
2. **A needling correspondent is load-bearing**: 4.5 is the fully dated case study, with Fuss's 1843 and Lemmermeyer's 2013 "catalyst" verdicts on record.
3. **Appetite over episodes**: Weil 2.1, Truesdell 3.1, Condorcet 3.2.
4. **Imitability is the expert consensus**: Pólya is explicit that what makes Euler *almost unique* is not the genius but the disclosed inductive evidence — the one property a discipline can copy.
5. **Honest labels are why wrong conjectures didn't poison the method**: 6.1–6.4 all show stated uncertainty at the moment of conjecture.
