# The Folklore Firewall — what this plugin refuses to repeat as fact

A plugin about honest labels cannot have dishonest anecdotes. The research pass
(dossiers II–IV in [`research/`](../research/)) provenance-checked the famous Euler stories;
these are the verdicts, and they **bind the mode**: items below are either never repeated, or
repeated only with their honest provenance attached.

## Debunked outright

- **The Euler–Diderot "algebraic proof of God"** ("Monsieur, (a+bⁿ)/n = x, donc Dieu existe —
  répondez!"). **Apocryphal.** The sole source, Thiébault (1804), names no Euler ("a Russian
  philosopher"), writes the formula differently, and explicitly disclaims the story. De Morgan
  (1872) inserted Euler's name and invented the courtroom theatrics; Bell and others propagated
  it. Diderot was mathematically competent (published on vibrating strings, pendulums,
  involutes). Debunked: B.H. Brown, *AMM* 49 (1942); R.J. Gillings, *AMM* 61 (1954). If it
  comes up, the mode debunks it kindly.

## Twentieth-century compressions (the idea is real; the quote is not)

- **"Now I will have fewer distractions"** (on losing his right eye). First documented in
  Howard Eves, *In Mathematical Circles* (1969) — 186 years late. The genuine 1783 ancestor is
  third-person: Condorcet wrote that "the still-more-absolute absence of all distraction... made
  him gain more than the weakening of his sight could make him lose," and Fuss says the same.
  Cite the observation, not the quip.
- **"He wrote papers between the first and second calls to dinner."** E.T. Bell (1937), no
  primary source. The documented basis for "worked anywhere, anytime" is Thiébault: "A child on
  the knees, a cat on his back, that's how he wrote his immortal works."
- **"He wrote faster than they could print."** No 18th-century source for the sentence; the
  documented substance is the Orlov promise (enough memoirs for twenty years — Fuss) and the
  actual ~fifty-year posthumous publication run.

## Hearsay — usable only with provenance attached

- **Laplace: "Read Euler, read Euler, he is the master of us all."** Reported by Guglielmo
  Libri (1846), claiming to have heard it from Laplace personally. No written Laplace source —
  and Libri was a convicted book thief and known forger. Usable as "reported by Libri," never
  as a bare Laplace quote.
- **Gauss: "The study of Euler's works will remain the best school..."** Authentic-looking but
  secondary-sourced (Fellmann 1995; Dunnington 1955); the original letter locus was not
  verified. Cite as secondhand-stable.

## Garbled details (true story, wrong numbers)

- **"Euler settled his students' dispute at the 50th decimal place."** Condorcet's actual
  account: two students summed a series *to the 17th term*; their results differed by one unit
  in the 50th figure; Euler redid the whole calculation in his head and settled it. Close, but
  quote Condorcet's version.
- **"Euler computed 20 digits of π in an hour."** No support in the primary record: in E705 he
  computed the two component series to 12 and 17 places and *never added them together*; E706
  contains no computation; no timing claim exists anywhere. Folklore.
- **"Fewer-known misattributions:** the "as men breathe" line — "Euler calculated without
  apparent effort, as men breathe, as eagles sustain themselves in the wind" — is **Arago's**
  (1841), not Condorcet's; even Gautschi's excellent SIAM article gets this wrong. Cite Arago.

## Technical corrections (commonly misstated in secondary sources)

- **e^(iπ) + 1 = 0:** Euler never wrote the identity in that exact form (Sandifer documents
  this at length). The mode's own banner shows it and says so when asked.
- **f(x)** is introduced in **E45** (the Additamentum, presented 1734), not E44.
- **The Fermat-number divisor form k·2^(n+2)+1** is **Lucas (1878)**; Euler's proven form
  (E134) is k·2^(n+1)+1.
- **"ζ(2) to 20 places in E25":** the method is E25's; the 20-place computations (ζ(2) and
  ζ(3)) are in **E47**. E41's printed value contains a dropped-digit typo.
- **"γ to 16 digits in E43":** E43 (1734) gives six printed digits (five correct); the
  15–16-digit computation is E212 (1755).
- **The γ symbol** is probably **Bretschneider's** (1835/37), not Euler's or Mascheroni's
  (both used other letters).
- **The Königsberg letters are frequently swap-attributed:** "so banal, but... worthy of
  attention" is from the letter to **Marinoni**; "bears little relationship to mathematics" is
  from the letter to **Ehler**. And E53 contains no graph drawing — the dots-and-lines picture
  is a 19th-century addition; Euler proved necessity only (sufficiency: Hierholzer, 1873).
- **The Lagrange courtesy** ("Euler withheld his own paper so young Lagrange could claim the
  calculus of variations"): the generosity is documented (Euler's crediting, the delayed
  publication order); the specific withheld-paper mechanism comes from Rouse Ball and has been
  disputed (Galletto 1989). Tell it with the dispute attached.
- **Odd perfect numbers:** Euler made no wrong conjecture here — he *proved* the Euclid–Euler
  theorem and a structure theorem for the odd case. (His documented wrong conjectures — 36
  officers, sum of powers — were labeled unproven at birth; see
  [The-Ledger](The-Ledger.md).)
